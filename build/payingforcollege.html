What is a
Federal Loan?
A private student loan is a financing option where the loans are made by an institution such as a bank, credit union, or college rather than the federal government.

Pros of Federal Loans

Have lower interest rates â€¨than private loans.
Have favorable repayment options after graduation.
Do not require a credit check or collateral.
Can be consolidated
upon graduation.
Subsidized loans do not accrue interest while actively enrolled in school.

Cons of Federal Loans

Subsidized are limited in how much you can borrow; given in increasing amounts until you hit your 3rd year in college then hit max amount you can receive ($21,000 total for 4 years).
Unsubsidized are somewhat limited in how much you can borrow; depends on how much your college cost and what year you are; the Student Aid Report will tell you your limit.
Can be stuck at a higher interest rate than your credit score (essentially you have better credit and could have qualified for a better interest rate than you are paying).

How to Apply

1

Submit your FAFSA

  • FAFSA is the Free Application for Federal Student Aid. In order to receive any federal student aid such as federal grants, work-study, and federal loans, you must complete the FAFSA.
  • You have to submit a FAFSA annually.
  • To see if you qualify for federal student aid, check out this list here.
  • You can list up to 10 institutions (if undecided where you will be a ending school)
  • Parental tax return - submit the prior year's taxes
2

Receive your Student Aid Report (SAR)

  • Once you submit FAFSA, you'll receive your SAR within 7-10 business days.
  • Check to make sure your informa on on your SAR is complete and accurate. If something isn't correct, you need to complete or correct your FAFSA ASAP.
  • Your SAR also lets you know what your Expected Family Contribution (EFC). This lets you know much you and your family are expected to pay each year for you to attend school.
  • Your SAR will be sent to all the schools your listed on your FAFSA. Each school will use your SAR to determine your eligibility for federal and nonfederal student aid.
3

Check Your Colleges Application Requirements

  • Each school has its own requirements for awarding financial aid, and may require you to submit more than just your FAFSA.
  • Check with each school's financial aid office to learn how to apply for their financial aid.
4

Receieve Your Award Letter

  • Each school will send you a financial aid award letter that will tell you how much aid you will receive and how much you will be expected to pay.
  • Look through (especially w/ help of a financial aid counselor) to make sure you understand the offer and that all the information is correct.
  • Each school has it's own timing for sending out financial aid award letters, so contact your school's financial aid offce to find out when you can expect it to arrive.
5

Entrance Counseling

Before you can receive your first student loan dispursement, you must go through financial education to take out the loan. Check with your school's financial aid offce for entrance counseling requirements.
6

Acceptance of Your Financial Aid Package

  • Your award letter will tell you how to accept their financial aid offer.
  • Usually you will need to sign a promissory note, a legal document in which you promise to pay back all the money you borrow for your education, plus accrued interest and fees.
  • Ask your school's financial aid offce if there are any additional requirements.
What is a
Private Loan?
A private student loan is a financing option where the loans are made by an institution such as a bank, credit union, or college rather than the federal govenment.

Pros of Private Loans

Access to more money than you can get through federal loans,
which has a maximum amount that you can receive.

Cons of Private Loans

Higher interest rates than federal loans.
Sometimes private loans do not offer the same benefits as federal loans, like loan forgiveness in the event of borrower death.
Less repayment flexibility than federal loans.

How to Apply

1

Shop Around

Compare interest rates, payoff terms, customer service reviews, and whether you need to be making payments while still actively borrowing.
2

Decide If You Need a Cosigner

  • If you don't have a credit history, or if your credit score is low, you might need to have a cosigner, like your parents.
  • Look for a company that offers cosigner release after a period of on time payments.
3

Check Your College's application requirements

Each private student loan applicatton has its own requirements and process, so ask the lender for all additional requirements.
  • Letter from your financial aid office certifying you will need a private student loan to cover costs
  • Tax documents
  • Income and bank statements
  • FAFSA award package
  • Identity verification
What is a
529 Plan?
A 529 plan is a tax-advantaged savings plan designed to encourage saving for future college costs.There are two types of 529 plans: pre-paid tuition plans and college savings plans. Every state has at least one type of 529 plan.

Pros of a 529 Plan

Earnings and withdrawals used for qualified higher education expenses are not subject to federal tax, and in most cases state tax as well.
Minimum monthly contibutions can be as low as $15 in some states.
The beneficary can be changed at any time to another member of the beneficiary's family (out to first cousins).
Can be used at any eligible educational institution in the U.S. or abroad.
Check here for eligible education institutions.
The money can be used for a variety of college expenses, including tuition, fees, room, board, books, supplies, and required equipment.

Cons of a 529 Plan

Withdrawing money for a non-qualified higher education expenses will be hit with a 10% penalty and will need to be reported as taxable income.

How to Apply

1

Shop Around

Every 529 Plan is a different, so read the official program disclosure statement for the plans you are interested in. You'll learn about the program's rules, how to contribute and withdraw funds, investment options, and other important information like fees and expenses.
2

Name an Account Beneficiary

  • This is the person who will be using the funds. This is not the same as the Account Owner.
  • You'll need the beneficiary's social security number, birth date, and current address.
  • You can easily change the beneficiary, as long as the new beneficiary is another member of the beneficiary's family (out to first cousins). If you are setting up the fund for a child who is not born yet, you can name yourself as the beneficiary and then change it once the child is born.
3

Name the Account Owner

The Account Owner retains control of the assets, so it is recommended that the account owner is a parent or grandparent to ensure the funds will be used towards college.
3

Add Funds

  • Some plans have minimum ini al contribu on amounts, which can be as low as $15.
  • Most plans will allow you to set automa c contribu ons straight from your checking or savings account, so you don't need to remember to do it manually each month.
  • Anyone can contribute to a 529 plan, so let your family members know about the account. This can make for a great (and useful!) holiday or birthday gift .
What is a
Scholarship?
A scholarship is money awarded to students for academic or other achievements that can be used towards college or career school, and does not have to be repaid.

Pros of a Scholarship

Scholarships do not have to be repaid. The more you apply for, the better. Even the smaller scholarships are well worth the long application process. A $500 scholarship you receive today, is $500 + interest you won't have to pay back in the future.

Cons of a Scholarship

While financial aid is usually based on financial need, there are many different scholarships with a wide range of qualifiers. Awards are available for students with academic, artistic, or athletic talent, as well as for particular fields of study and underrepresented groups.

How to Apply

1

Search for Scholarships

There are many scholarship databases available online, and several of them are free to use. Use one of the free databases to search for awards that meet your background and qualifications.
2

Apply, Apply, Apply!

  • There are over 1.5 million scholarships available. The more scholarships you apply for, the more potential awards you will receive.
  • Read each application thoroughly to ensure you send in all the required paperwork by the due date.
3

Keep Up the Good Work

Be sure to keep up your grades and extracurricular activities. Most scholarship awards have requirements such as maintaining your GPA,
What is a Direct
PLUS Loan?
Direct PLUS Loans are federal public loans offered to graduate/professional students, or to parents of undergrad students to cover costs not included in other financial aid. Parent PLUS Loans are loans that parents can borrow to help pay for their child's education.

Pros of Direct PLUS Loans

Payments can be deferred until after the child has left college (for loans made after July 1, 2008).
Fixed interest rates
won't raise for the duration of the loan.
Debt will be canceled
if the parent or the student dies or becomes disabled.

Cons of Direct PLUS Loans

Interest builds even during payment deferment.
PLUS loans have limited repayment options, but more than private parent loans.
For borrowers with good credit scores, Direct PLUS loans usually have a higher interest rate than private parent loans.

How to Apply

1

Ask Yourself: "Is This the Right Choice for Our Family?"

  • Only you and your family can decide if this is the right option.
  • Some questions to ask yourselves are
  • Can we afford this debt? Take into consideration your other financial obligations such as your mortgage, if you still have your own student loan debt, and what your other long term financial goals are.
  • Can we continue this trend if we have more than one child?
  • Why is the cost of this education higher than the available financial aid? Is public school a more realistic option for our family?"
2

Call the Financial Aid Office

Every college has different requirements for applying for a PLUS loan, so check with your child's financial aid office to determine eligibility and requirements.
